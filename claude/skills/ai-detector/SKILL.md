# AI Writing Detector Skill

## Purpose
Analyze text to assess the likelihood it was generated by an AI model. Provide a
likelihood rating and flag specific evidence.

## How to Use This Skill
When asked to evaluate text for AI generation, follow the analysis framework below.
Output a structured assessment with a likelihood rating and specific flagged evidence.

## Analysis Framework

### Structural Tells
- **Overly clean structure**: Problem → Example → Solution → Conclusion with no
  deviation. AI tends to follow predictable rhetorical templates.
- **Symmetric balance**: Every point has a counterpoint. Every pro has a con. Real
  writers are messier and more opinionated.
- **Formulaic paragraph length**: Paragraphs of similar length throughout, especially
  3-4 sentences each.
- **Tidy summary sentences**: Closing sentences that neatly restate everything just
  said, e.g. "So in summary, X is important because Y and Z."

### Phrase-Level Tells
Flag any of the following patterns:
- Opening with agreement: "Great question", "That's a great point", "I really liked
  how you..."
- Transitional filler: "It's worth noting that", "It's important to understand that",
  "This highlights the fact that"
- Hedging openers: "While X is true, it's also important to consider Y"
- Abstract wisdom phrases: "being intentional with it", "with greater accountability",
  "in a controlled way", "channels it effectively"
- Rhetorical meta-commentary: "The distinction I would draw is this:", "To put it
  another way:", "What this really comes down to is:"
- Overly formal closers: "Overall, X demonstrates Y", "In essence, X and Y work
  together to Z"

### Content Tells
- **Generic scenario construction**: Specific-sounding but substanceless examples.
  "Imagine a mid-sized healthcare company..." or "Consider a fintech team building a
  payment system..." with no actual technical detail.
- **Authoritative citations appearing conveniently**: References that appear exactly
  where needed with suspiciously complete bibliographic detail.
- **Lack of personal specificity**: Claims of personal experience with no concrete
  details. "In my experience on a project..." followed by a textbook description.
- **Surface-level technical depth**: Correct terminology used in ways that don't
  demonstrate actual hands-on knowledge. Mentions Swagger docs without knowing what
  a Swagger doc actually looks like.
- **Suspiciously comprehensive coverage**: Every aspect of a topic addressed with
  equal weight. Real experts have opinions about what matters more.

### Style Tells
- Consistent sentence variety that feels engineered rather than natural
- No contractions in formal writing but perfect grammar throughout
- Vocabulary slightly above register for the context
- No typos, false starts, or informal asides
- Transitions between paragraphs that are too smooth
- **Em dashes (—)**: A widely recognized AI tell. Frequent em dashes for parentheticals,
  appositives, or dramatic pauses are a strong signal, especially in casual or
  short-form writing where most humans would reach for a comma, colon, or parentheses.
  A single em dash in a long document is weak evidence; multiple em dashes, or any
  em dash in informal writing, is strong evidence. Note: hyphens (-) and en dashes (–)
  do not count.

## Output Format

Provide your assessment in this format:

```
AI Likelihood: [Low / Moderate / High / Almost Certain]

Evidence:
- [Specific phrase or pattern flagged, with quote if possible]
- [...]

Notes:
[Any context that affects the assessment, e.g. "personal anecdote adds credibility"
or "technical specificity is consistent throughout which reduces likelihood"]
```

## Calibration Notes

- A single tell is not enough. Look for patterns.
- Personal technical detail (specific tools, error messages, workarounds) strongly
  reduces AI likelihood.
- Typos, grammatical quirks, and informal asides strongly reduce AI likelihood.
- A well-written human can produce clean prose. Clean prose alone is not a tell.
- Academic writing naturally has some AI-like patterns. Context matters.
- AI writing about AI topics tends to be more convincing because the training data
  is dense in that area. Be more skeptical of clean AI/ML writing.
